# Study Guide Authoring Rules

_Last updated: 2026-04-20_

These rules govern every chapter-by-chapter Bible study guide on Learn of
Christ — the Genesis 1 deep-dive, the chapter-content aggregator entries, any
new rich commentary component, and any future AI-generated batch. If you are
an agent writing or revising study content, treat this document as binding.

## Audience

The study guides must be usable by **every Christian tradition** that reads
the Bible as Scripture. Specifically, they must be usable by **members of
The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (LDS)** without asking them
to assent to post-biblical Protestant theological conclusions.

Our audience includes, at minimum:

- Mainline and evangelical Protestants (Baptist, Methodist, Lutheran,
  Presbyterian, Pentecostal, Non-denominational, etc.)
- Roman Catholics
- Eastern Orthodox
- Seventh-day Adventists
- Latter-day Saints
- Jehovah's Witnesses
- Seekers, lapsed Christians, and the Christian-curious

## Invisibility Rule (Read First)

**These rules are internal and must never surface in any study guide.**
Do not mention LDS / Latter-day Saints, Protestant / non-denominational
positions, denominational neutrality, doctrinal flashpoints, "different
traditions interpret this differently," or any variant of "we're staying
neutral for readers of all backgrounds." The guide should simply read as
thoughtful, biblically grounded, Christ-centered commentary — never as
commentary that announces its own editorial constraints.

If neutrality is required, achieve it by *what you write and don't write*,
never by narrating the choice.

## The Core Rule

**When Protestant / non-denominational conclusions conflict with LDS
doctrine, err on the side of letting the reader draw their own conclusion
from the text.** Don't bake a sectarian conclusion into the commentary.

Vagueness is fine when a tradition-specific conclusion would otherwise
alienate part of the audience. Stick to what the text itself says, what
first-century Christians could reasonably have understood, and what multiple
traditions can genuinely affirm.

The LDS "Lens" feature (via `DenominationContext`) is where tradition-
specific framing belongs — not the base commentary. The default commentary
should pass a test: an LDS theologian and a Baptist pastor should both be
able to read it and find it truthful, even if each would add or emphasize
different things.

## Specific Doctrinal Flashpoints

Below are well-known points where Protestant default framings conflict with
LDS doctrine. For each, the rule is: **don't assert the Protestant position
as the conclusion.** State the text. Say what Christians have historically
drawn from it. Leave room for both.

### 1. Ex Nihilo vs. Organization from Pre-existing Matter

- **Protestant default:** God created the universe *ex nihilo*, from nothing.
- **LDS:** God *organized* pre-existing, eternal matter. Intelligence is
  also eternal and uncreated.
- **Rule:** On Genesis 1:1 and similar passages, say "God brought the world
  into being" or "God created the heavens and the earth" — don't insist on
  "from absolute nothing." The Hebrew *bara'* does not by itself require
  *ex nihilo*. Describe the verb, note both historical readings, and stop.

### 2. Trinity

- **Protestant default:** One God in three co-substantial Persons, as defined
  at Nicaea / Chalcedon.
- **LDS:** Father, Son, and Holy Spirit are three divine personages acting
  as one Godhead. They reject post-biblical philosophical definitions
  (Nicene creed, *homoousios*, "without body, parts, or passions").
- **Rule:** Affirm what the Bible affirms — Father, Son, Spirit all divine,
  all present at creation, working together. Don't invoke "one substance,"
  "two natures in one person," Chalcedonian language, or Nicene philosophical
  categories as if they were the text's own framing. Say "Father, Son, and
  Spirit together" rather than "the three persons of the one God" etc.

### 3. God's Embodiment

- **Protestant default:** God is pure spirit, "without body, parts, or
  passions," immaterial.
- **LDS:** The Father has an embodied glorified form; spirit is refined
  matter.
- **Rule:** Don't say God is "immaterial" or "non-physical" or "has no
  body." When Scripture uses anthropomorphic language ("the face of God,"
  "the hand of God"), present it plainly. Let the reader's tradition tell
  them whether to allegorize it.

### 4. The Great Apostasy / Restoration

- **Protestant default:** The apostolic / orthodox church has persisted
  through history (Catholic, Orthodox, or Reformation continuation).
- **LDS:** A Great Apostasy followed the death of the apostles; authority
  was restored through Joseph Smith.
- **Rule:** Don't frame church history as unbroken or frame any particular
  denomination as "the continuation of apostolic Christianity." Keep
  claims tied to what the biblical authors and the first-century community
  held. Avoid "the Church has always believed X" unless X is unambiguously
  biblical.

### 5. Pre-mortal Existence of Souls

- **Protestant default:** Souls are newly created at conception (or
  implied to begin at birth).
- **LDS:** Human spirits are eternal intelligences organized by God before
  mortal life.
- **Rule:** Don't assert that a soul's existence *begins* at conception or
  at birth. When creation texts describe humans being made, stay close to
  the text's own wording ("formed of dust," "breath of life") without
  philosophical add-ons about when personal existence starts.

### 6. Total Depravity / Irresistible Grace / Predestination

- **Protestant default (esp. Reformed):** Humans are totally depraved since
  the Fall; salvation depends on God's sovereign election; grace is
  irresistible.
- **LDS:** Humans are genuine free agents. The Fall did not incapacitate
  human moral agency. Grace is offered universally but requires covenant
  cooperation.
- **Rule:** Don't frame humans as incapable of any good or salvation as
  wholly monergistic (God-alone-does-it). Don't frame election as
  unconditional. Affirm what the text affirms: human responsibility AND
  God's initiative, side by side.

### 7. The Fortunate Fall

- **Protestant default:** The Fall is purely a tragedy that needed to be
  undone.
- **LDS:** The Fall was part of God's plan — "Adam fell that men might be;
  and men are, that they might have joy" (2 Ne. 2:25).
- **Rule:** Don't frame Genesis 3 as only disaster. You can note that
  Christians have read it both as catastrophe and as part of a larger
  trajectory toward redemption. Lean on the text's own promises (e.g.
  Gen 3:15) rather than on a purely pessimistic reading.

### 8. Sola Scriptura / Canon Closed

- **Protestant default:** The Bible alone is sufficient; canon is closed.
- **LDS and Roman Catholic:** Ongoing revelation / magisterial authority
  matters too.
- **Rule:** Don't assert "the Bible alone" or "no further revelation is
  possible." You can point to Scripture's authority without claiming
  exclusivity. Avoid closing off possibilities the biblical text itself
  does not close off.

### 9. Christology: One Nature vs. Two Natures

- **Protestant / Orthodox / Catholic default:** Christ has two natures in
  one person (Chalcedonian duophysitism).
- **LDS:** Christ has one nature, simultaneously fully human and fully
  divine — because for Latter-day Saints human and divine are not mutually
  exclusive categories.
- **Rule:** Affirm Jesus as both fully human and fully God — the biblical
  witness. Don't invoke "two natures" as a required theological frame.

### 10. Deification / Theosis

- **Protestant default (esp. Reformed):** The creator-creature distinction
  is absolute; humans cannot become "as God."
- **LDS / Eastern Orthodox:** Humans can be made like God (theosis,
  deification; 2 Peter 1:4; John 10:34–35; Psalm 82:6; C. S. Lewis).
- **Rule:** When the text says humans are made in God's image, being
  conformed to Christ's likeness, etc. — let that stand. Don't insert
  "of course this does not mean we become divine" or similar disclaimers.
  Let readers of different traditions fill in what they believe the
  destiny of the redeemed is.

### 11. Ministry to the Dead (1 Peter 3:18–19; 4:6)

- **Protestant default:** Often spiritualized or treated as ambiguous.
- **LDS:** Christ literally ministered to departed spirits; the living
  perform vicarious ordinances (baptism for the dead).
- **Rule:** When these passages come up, present what the text says. Don't
  flatten the passage into "this just means X metaphorically." Leave the
  implications open.

### 12. Marriage / Family in the Eternities

- **Protestant default:** Marriage is for this life only (often read from
  Matt 22:30).
- **LDS:** Sealed family relationships continue eternally.
- **Rule:** Present Matt 22:30 in its context — it answers a trap question
  about Levirate marriage, not a general pronouncement on eternal
  relationships. Don't use it as a proof-text to close the door on eternal
  family bonds.

### 13. Celibacy and Asceticism

- **Protestant / Catholic (some):** Celibacy can be a higher calling.
- **LDS:** Physical creation, marriage, and the body are good; no celibate
  ideal.
- **Rule:** Don't frame body, marriage, or physical creation as lesser than
  "pure spirit." Affirm the goodness of the physical creation (Gen 1:31).

### 14. Cross-Reference Citations

- It's fine to cross-reference the New Testament, especially Pauline and
  Johannine writings, when drawing Christ-connections.
- It's fine to quote C. S. Lewis, early church fathers (Irenaeus,
  Athanasius, etc.) where they express ideas the whole audience shares.
- Do **not** cite post-Reformation Protestant theologians (Calvin, Edwards,
  Piper, etc.) as *definitional authorities* on what a passage means. Cite
  them only as one reader among many if needed, never as the final word.
- Do **not** quote LDS-specific scripture (Book of Mormon, D&C,
  Pearl of Great Price) in the base commentary. Save that for the LDS
  Lens when it exists.

## Tone

- Reverent. Literary. Christ-centered.
- Confident where the biblical text is clear.
- Humble and open-ended where the text is ambiguous or where traditions
  differ.
- Pastoral. Speak to the reader's life.
- Never triumphalist about a single tradition.

## Quick Self-Check Before Shipping a Guide

Before you commit a new or revised study guide, ask:

1. Have I said anything as certain that a thoughtful LDS reader would
   reject on doctrinal grounds?
2. Have I framed the Fall as *purely* a tragedy, with no sense of trajectory?
3. Have I used "the three persons of the one God," "two natures," "pure
   spirit," "ex nihilo," "sola scriptura," "total depravity," or other
   hellenistic / post-Nicene / Reformation-coded phrases?
4. Have I cited a post-Reformation theologian as the decisive interpreter?
5. Have I closed off a reading the text itself leaves open?
6. Have I, anywhere in the guide, *talked about* these rules —
   mentioned denominations, Latter-day Saints, Protestant positions,
   neutrality, or the existence of different traditions? If yes,
   **delete that sentence.** (See Invisibility Rule above.)

If the answer to any of these is "yes," rewrite.

## Where Tradition-Specific Content Lives

The app has a **Denomination Lens** feature
(`src/lib/DenominationContext.tsx`) that renders tradition-specific framing
when the user opts in. That is the correct place for:

- Specifically Protestant / Reformed conclusions
- Specifically Catholic / sacramental conclusions
- Specifically LDS / Restoration conclusions
- Specifically Adventist, Pentecostal, JW, etc. conclusions

The base commentary stays neutral and text-driven. The Lens layer is where
a reader sees their own tradition speak. This separation is what lets the
default guides serve everyone.
